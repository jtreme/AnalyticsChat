Section 1
Residuals: what OLS leaves behind
Actual value
Y
Predicted value
Ŷ
Residual
ê = Y − Ŷ
The residual for each observation is the vertical distance between the actual data point and the fitted regression line. A positive residual means the model underpredicted. A negative residual means the model overpredicted. OLS chooses the line that makes these as small as possible in total — specifically, it minimizes Σê².
Good news: The OLS residuals always sum to exactly zero — positive and negative residuals cancel out. This is why we need SSE (squared residuals) to measure fit.
Watch out: Residuals are not the same as the true errors (ε). The true errors are unobservable population quantities. Residuals are their sample estimates.

Section 4
Outliers vs high-leverage points
Outlier
A point with a large residual — the model fits it poorly. It has an unusual Y value given its X. May or may not strongly influence the regression line depending on where it sits on X.
High leverage
A point with an unusual X value — far from the mean of X. It has the potential to pull the regression line strongly, even if its residual looks small. The dangerous combination is high leverage + large residual.
Baseline: clean data. The OLS line (blue) fits well.

Section 1
BLUE — Best Linear Unbiased Estimator
B
Best
Minimum variance among all linear unbiased estimators. OLS produces the tightest estimates — no other unbiased linear method does better.
L
Linear
Linear in the coefficients, not the variables. A model with X², log(X), or dummy variables is still linear in the β's — OLS still applies.
U
Unbiased
E(β̂) = β. Across many samples, the average OLS estimate equals the true population value. Not right every time — right on average.
E
Estimator
A rule or procedure for computing an estimate from data. OLS is one estimator — it finds the line that minimizes SSE.
The key distinction: B and U are separate. Heteroskedasticity breaks B (standard errors are wrong) but not U (estimates are still unbiased). Omitted Variable Bias — OVB — breaks U (estimates are systematically wrong). Breaking U is more serious — you are estimating the wrong thing entirely.

Section 3
Omitted Variable Bias (OVB) — the intuition
The scenario: You want to know the effect of fertilizer on crop yield. You run:
Yield = β₀ + β₁·Fertilizer + ε

But you forgot to include irrigation. Irrigated farms produce more yield. And irrigated farms also tend to apply more fertilizer. So irrigation ends up in the error term ε.
What OLS sees
Farms with more fertilizer have higher yield. OLS assigns all of that to fertilizer — including the part that actually came from irrigation. The fertilizer coefficient is too high.
What is really happening
Fertilizer raises yield AND irrigation raises yield. Because they are correlated, OLS cannot separate them. Irrigation's effect bleeds into the fertilizer coefficient.
Two conditions required for OVB
1. The omitted variable affects Y (irrigation affects yield)
2. The omitted variable is correlated with an included X (irrigation correlated with fertilizer)
If either condition fails — no OVB. A variable that affects Y but is uncorrelated with X just makes your model less precise (goes into ε randomly). That is not bias.
Section 4
Which direction is the bias?
The rule
Bias direction = sign(effect of omitted on Y) × sign(correlation of omitted with X)
Positive × Positive = Upward bias  |  Positive × Negative = Downward bias
Negative × Positive = Downward bias  |  Negative × Negative = Upward bias

Section 1
The OLS requirement: no perfect collinearity

For OLS to work at all, the predictor variables cannot be perfectly linearly related. This is one of the core conditions — when it is violated, the X'X matrix is singular and OLS literally cannot be computed.

Perfect collinearity — OLS fails
One predictor is an exact linear combination of others. The X'X matrix is singular — no inverse exists. OLS cannot produce any estimate.
Examples:
• Dummy trap: Spring+Summer+Fall+Winter = 1 = intercept
• Including the same variable twice
• One variable = 2 × another variable
Near-perfect collinearity — OLS works but struggles
Predictors are highly (but not perfectly) correlated. OLS can be computed. But standard errors are large and estimates are unstable.
Examples:
• Fertilizer & irrigation correlated at r=0.92
• Height in inches & height in cm
• GDP and GDP per capita
You have already seen perfect multicollinearity — in the dummy variable module. Including all k season dummies plus an intercept triggers exactly this: the four dummies always sum to 1, identically equal to the intercept column. That is why Excel cannot estimate one of them and shows #N/A. Now you know the deeper reason why: OLS cannot invert X'X when one column is a perfect linear combination of others — infinitely many coefficient combinations fit equally well, so there is no unique solution.
The spectrum of collinearity: The dummy trap is the extreme end — perfect, exact collinearity. OLS fails completely. This module covers the full spectrum: from perfect (OLS cannot compute) to near-perfect (OLS computes but standard errors explode) to mild (no problem). The key distinction is whether the X'X matrix is singular (perfect — cannot invert) or merely ill-conditioned (near-perfect — can invert but results are imprecise).

Section 5
Multicollinearity vs OVB — the contrast that matters
Omitted Variable Bias
You left out a variable that belongs and is correlated with included predictors.

→ Coefficients are biased — wrong on average
→ More data does not fix it
→ Fix: add the omitted variable — when it is observable and measurable. When it is not (e.g. unobserved ability, unobserved farm quality), fixing OVB requires more advanced methods covered in higher-level courses.
Multicollinearity
You included variables that are highly correlated with each other.

→ Coefficients are unbiased but imprecise
→ More data helps (larger n shrinks SEs)
→ Fix: more data, or accept wide CIs
The cruel irony: fixing OVB by adding the correlated variable often introduces multicollinearity. You go from biased-and-precise to unbiased-but-imprecise. That trade is almost always worth making — a biased estimate is systematically wrong no matter how large your sample, while an imprecise estimate gets better with more data.
Section 6
What to do about multicollinearity
Good options
1. Collect more data — larger n shrinks SEs even with high VIF. More observations give the data more chances to vary independently.

2. Drop a redundant variable — only if it is truly measuring the same thing as another (e.g. height in inches and height in cm). Document the decision.

3. Combine variables — create a composite index (e.g. "input intensity" = weighted sum of fertilizer + irrigation). Useful when both measure the same underlying concept.

4. Accept the imprecision — if both variables belong theoretically, report wide CIs honestly. Wide CIs are informative — they tell readers the data cannot separate these effects.
What NOT to do
Do not drop a variable just because VIF is high — if it belongs in the model, dropping it creates OVB. Trade bias for imprecision only if the variable is redundant.

Do not ignore it — high VIF with wide CIs means your individual coefficient estimates are unreliable. Do not over-interpret them.

Do not mistake it for bias — multicollinearity does not bias your estimates. Reporting "the coefficient might be biased due to multicollinearity" is incorrect.

Do not use stepwise selection — dropping variables based on p-values in the presence of multicollinearity produces unreliable results.

Section 1
Heteroskedasticity — non-constant error variance

OLS assumes the error term has constant variance across all observations — this is called homoskedasticity. When variance is not constant, we have heteroskedasticity (hetero = different, skedastic = scatter).

Homoskedastic (good)

Residuals have constant spread at all levels of X. OLS assumptions met.

Heteroskedastic (funnel)

Residuals spread out as X increases. Classic funnel pattern.

You have already seen this. In the residual analysis module, the funnel pattern was one of the four diagnostic patterns. Now you learn exactly what it means, why it matters, and what to do about it.
Agribusiness examples where you expect it
Farm revenue vs farm size — larger farms earn more on average but also face more variable outcomes: more crops, more market exposure, more weather risk.
Crop yield vs fertilizer — at low fertilizer levels there is little room for variation; at high levels, yield varies widely depending on soil, water, and management.
Commodity prices over time — price volatility tends to increase during periods of high prices (supply shocks, speculation).
Herd size vs veterinary costs — small herds have predictable costs; large herds have highly variable costs depending on disease outbreaks.
Section 2
What heteroskedasticity breaks — and what it does not
Still works — Unbiased
OLS coefficients are still E(β̂) = β. On average across many samples, OLS gives the correct answer. Heteroskedasticity does not push estimates systematically high or low.
Broken — Best & inference
OLS is no longer the minimum-variance estimator. More importantly: the standard errors Excel reports are wrong — making t-statistics, p-values, and confidence intervals unreliable.
Why the standard errors are wrong
OLS computes SEs assuming Var(ε) = σ² (constant for all observations). When variance actually differs by observation — say, large farms have much larger ε than small farms — OLS uses the wrong formula. It typically underestimates SE when variance grows with X, making t-statistics look too large and p-values too small. You may conclude a predictor is significant when it is not.
ProblemCoefficientsStandard errorsFix
HeteroskedasticityUnbiased ✓Wrong ✗Robust SEs
OVBBiased ✗Correct (given model) ✓Add the variable
Near-multicollinearityUnbiased ✓Inflated ✗More data
A note before reaching for robust SEs: Heteroskedasticity sometimes signals a deeper problem — wrong functional form or a missing variable — rather than just a variance structure issue. If a funnel pattern disappears when you log Y or add a missing predictor, the right fix was the model, not the standard errors. Robust SEs correct the inference but leave the underlying model unchanged. Always ask why the pattern is there before deciding how to respond.

Section 4
Detecting heteroskedasticity
Method 1 — Visual (residual plot)
Plot residuals vs fitted values (or vs each X). Look for:
Funnel opening right — variance grows with X
Funnel opening left — variance shrinks with X
Bow-tie / diamond — variance peaks in the middle
Quick and intuitive. Always do this first. May miss subtle heteroskedasticity with noisy data.
Method 2 — Breusch-Pagan test
Formal test. Regresses squared residuals (ê²) on the predictors. If predictors explain ê², variance is not constant.

H₀: Homoskedasticity (constant variance)
H₁: Heteroskedasticity
Reject H₀ if p < α
More powerful than visual inspection for subtle patterns. Requires running a second regression on the residuals.
Funnel: variance increases with fitted values. Classic sign of heteroskedasticity in cross-sectional data.
Section 5
The fix — robust (White) standard errors
Robust standard errors (also called White standard errors, HC standard errors, or sandwich standard errors) correct the variance-covariance matrix of the OLS estimator to account for non-constant variance. The OLS coefficients are unchanged — only the SEs, t-statistics, p-values, and CIs change.

The original SE was overstating precision. The robust SE is the honest answer — Acres is genuinely important, just not as precisely estimated as the OLS SE suggested.
In Excel: Excel's built-in regression tool does not compute robust SEs. In practice, students use R, Stata, or Python for robust SEs. For this course, the key is knowing when they are needed and how to interpret the change.
Section 6
Log transformation as an alternative fix
Connection to the log specifications module: Remember the power function Y = A·Xβ? When the true relationship is a power function, the errors are multiplicative — they scale proportionally with the level of Y. Large farms don't just have higher revenue, they have proportionally more variable revenue. Taking log(Y) converts those multiplicative errors into additive ones — stabilizing the variance. So logging does two things at once: it linearizes the power function relationship AND fixes the heteroskedasticity. One transformation, two problems solved.
When does this apply? If your revenue data follows Revenue = A·Acresβ with multiplicative errors (ε scales with Revenue), then the raw model has both a functional form problem (curve vs line) and a heteroskedasticity problem (variance grows with Y). The log-log model log(Revenue) = β₀ + β₁·log(Acres) solves both: it linearizes the power function and produces approximately constant error variance in log space.
Residuals from Revenue ~ Acres (funnel)
Residuals from log(Revenue) ~ log(Acres) (stabilized)
Important caveat: This only works when the errors are truly multiplicative — i.e. when the true relationship is a power function or something close to it. If heteroskedasticity has a different source (e.g. measurement error concentrated in a subgroup, or an omitted group variable), logging may not stabilize the variance. Always re-check the residual plot after transformation.
Robust SEs vs log transformation — when to use each
Use robust SEs when: you need coefficients in original units, the log transformation is not theoretically motivated, or heteroskedasticity is mild and you just need correct inference.
Use log when: the true relationship is multiplicative (power function), you want elasticity interpretation, and you expect logging to stabilize variance — one transformation fixes both functional form and heteroskedasticity.

Section 1
Why log-transform variables?
Reason 1 — Diminishing returns
Many economic relationships are proportional, not linear. Each additional acre adds less revenue than the previous one. Log captures this naturally.
Reason 2 — Elasticities
Log-log coefficients are elasticities — directly comparable across goods and contexts. Essential for demand analysis, price sensitivity, supply response.
Reason 3 — Heteroskedasticity
When variance grows with the level of Y (common in revenue, wages, prices), log(Y) stabilizes the variance — connecting back to what you learned in the heteroskedasticity module.
The linear model forces a constant slope — each additional acre adds the same dollar amount. The log model allows diminishing returns — each additional acre adds proportionally less.

Section 3
Log-log — where the interpretation comes from
Step 1 — the true relationship is a power function
Many economic relationships follow a power function: Y = A · Xβ. For farm revenue vs acreage, each additional acre adds proportionally less revenue as the farm grows — diminishing returns when β < 1. This produces a curve in levels, not a straight line.
Revenue = A · Acresβ
The problem: OLS requires a linear model — it minimizes the sum of squared residuals from a straight line. A power function is not linear, so OLS cannot estimate A and β directly from Y = A·Xβ. We need to transform it into something OLS can handle.
Step 2 — the fix: take log of both sides
There is a mathematical property of logarithms that makes this work: log(Xβ) = β·log(X). The exponent comes down and becomes a multiplier. That turns a nonlinear power function into a linear equation. Watch:
Start:    Revenue = A · Acresβ
Log both sides:   log(Revenue) = log(A · Acresβ)
Split the log:    log(Revenue) = log(A) + log(Acresβ)
Exponent rule:   log(Revenue) = log(A) + β · log(Acres)
Rename:        log(Revenue) = β₀ + β₁ · log(Acres)
The nonlinear power function becomes linear in log space. OLS can now estimate β₀ and β₁ directly. And β₁ is the same β as in the original power function — it is the exponent.
Step 3 — still linear in coefficients → still BLUE
The model log(Y) = β₀ + β₁·log(X) is nonlinear in variables (X appears as log(X)) but linear in coefficients (β₀ and β₁ enter linearly). The L in BLUE means linear in coefficients — not in variables. You saw this same idea in the quadratic module: Y = β₀ + β₁X + β₂X² is nonlinear in X but linear in β₀, β₁, β₂. OLS applies to any model that is linear in its coefficients.
Step 4 — where β₁ × 100% comes from
Start from the estimated model and increase X by a small amount ΔX:
log(Ynew) − log(Yold) = β₁·log(X+ΔX) − β₁·log(X)
log(Ynew/Yold) = β₁·log((X+ΔX)/X)
log(Ynew/Yold) = β₁·log(1 + ΔX/X)
Now use the approximation: for small x, log(1+x) ≈ x. This is the same approximation that gives the 100·β rule:
log(Ynew/Yold) ≈ β₁ · (ΔX/X)
%ΔY ≈ β₁ · %ΔX
β₁ ≈ %ΔY / %ΔX ← definition of elasticity
This is exactly where 100·β comes from — and it is an approximation that works well when %ΔX is small (say, 1–5%). For larger changes (e.g. acreage doubles), use the exact formula: (Ynew/Yold − 1) × 100 = ((Xnew/Xold)β₁ − 1) × 100.

Section 6
Choosing the right specification
Theory first
What does economic theory predict? Demand curves → log-log for elasticity. Growth processes → log-linear. Diminishing returns → linear-log or log-log. Constant marginal product → linear-linear.
Then residuals
Run each plausible spec and examine residual plots. Does the log model have more constant variance? Does the arch in the linear model's residuals disappear in the log model? Let the residuals guide you.
Interpretability
Who is your audience? Economists: elasticities are natural. Managers: absolute dollar changes may be more intuitive. Match the spec to what the audience can use.
Cannot compare R²
R² from log(Y) and R² from Y are not comparable — they measure explained variation in different things. Use AIC/BIC or residual plots for model selection across different Y specifications.
